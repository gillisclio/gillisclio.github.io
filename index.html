The Significance of the Honeybee

Learn about the world's number one pollinators, their impact on the world, and why we need to save them.

Honeybee

Apis melliferia

The honeybee is the Earth’s most prolific polinator. They are highly social insects and live in colonies, ruled by a single queen bee (more on her later). The oldest record of the honeybee was found in Myanmar, and dates to 100 million years ago. However, the Ancient Egyptians were the very first apiarists, or bee keepers, and began to cultivate hives for honey. Today, we recognize the critical role that honeybees play in our lives–as pollinators of plants and of producers of honey!

The 3 Bees

A colony is made up of three different types of honeybees.

The Worker

Worker bees forage for pollen on local trees and flowers. Chances are, if you've seen a bee in the wild, it's been one of these lady bees. Yup, all worker bees are female!

The Queen

All hail the queen ~ A single queen honeybee is in charge of the entire hive. She has two jobs: to spawn the next generation of bees, and to produce chemicals that direct the other bees actions.

The Drone

Drone bees, all of which are male, exist to reproduce with the queen. Mating happens in the springtime and summer, and once winter hits, drones get kicked out of the hive!

What's More...

More bees, more money, more awareness

Honeybees polinate 70 of the around 100 crop species that feed 90% of the world. Just wow.

In fact, the presence and absence of honeybees tells us when there has been a shift in the ecological balance, which impacts food production.

The economic health of U.S. agriculture depends on this balance – honey bees are responsible for $30 billion a year in crops.

(Not to mention that almonds literally would not exist without honeybees to pollinate almond trees.)

Unfortunately, the future of the honeybee is at risk.

Threats to Honeybees

Colony Collapse

Colony Collapse Disorder or CCD takes places when a majority of worker bees leave a colony, leaving behind the queen and a few nurse bees to take care of the young larva. Some believe that CCD takes place due to mites, loss of habitat, and malnutrition.

Parasites

Various types of mites are particularly damaging to young honeybees. If a hive is invested with mites, its developing honeybees will typically have disjointed or damaged wings and smaller, abnormal abdomens, and the hive will die in one to two years.

Pesticides

Neuro-active insecticides, which are chemically similar to nicotine, are used on virtually all U.S. corn plants. Unfortunately have been linked to many adverse ecological effects, including CCD.
